The stability of benzene was a major puzzle for early chemists. According to August Kekulé’s 1865 proposal, benzene consisted of a hexagonal ring with alternating single and double bonds. However, experimental data consistently showed that benzene was far more stable and less reactive than a molecule with three double bonds should be. This extra stability is explained by two main concepts: Resonance and Delocalization.

The most concrete proof of benzene's stability comes from measuring the energy released when hydrogen is added to the molecule (hydrogenation).

* **Expected:** If benzene were simply 1,3,5-cyclohexatriene (three isolated double bonds), its heat of hydrogenation should be roughly three times that of cyclohexene (one double bond).
* **Cyclohexene:** $\approx -120 \text{ kJ/mol}$
* **Expected Benzene:** $3 \times (-120) = -360 \text{ kJ/mol}$
* **Actual:** The experimental value for benzene is only $-208 \text{ kJ/mol}$.

The difference of $152 \text{ kJ/mol}$ is known as the **Resonance Energy**. This energy "gap" represents how much more stable benzene is than the Kekulé structure predicts.

* **The "Donut" Clouds:** These six $p$-orbitals overlap sideways to form a continuous ring of electron density above and below the plane of the carbon atoms.
* **Bond Lengths:** Because the electrons are spread out, all carbon-carbon bonds in benzene are identical in length ($139 \text{ pm}$)—shorter than a single bond but longer than a double bond.

3. Chemical Reactivity

Breaking the "circle" of delocalized electrons to add new atoms would require a massive input of energy to overcome the resonance stability. By choosing substitution, the molecule keeps its stable, delocalized electron cloud intact.

Because gases have large intermolecular distances and can be easily compressed.

**Key Concept:** Gases are more compressible than liquids because they have larger intermolecular distances and can be easily compressed by reducing volume.

**Explanation:**

**1. Intermolecular Distances:**
Gases have larger intermolecular distances than liquids. Gases have weak intermolecular forces, which allow them to be easily compressed. Liquids have strong intermolecular forces that prevent significant compression.

**2. Elasticity:**
Gases are highly elastic; they can be easily compressed and expanded. When a gas is compressed, the intermolecular distances are reduced, causing the molecules to move closer together. When the pressure is released, the gas expands, returning to its original volume.

**3. Kinetic Energy:**
Gases have higher kinetic energy per molecule compared to liquids. The kinetic energy of gas molecules allows them to move freely and occupy less space when compressed.

**4. Pressure-Volume Relationship:**
The pressure-volume relationship for gases (Boyle's law) shows that a small change in volume results in a large change in pressure. For liquids, the pressure-volume relationship is much less pronounced (incompressible).
